English Literature

Marks come from: clear thesis, close analysis, and structure under time.

Papers

  • Paper 1: Unseen text analysis
  • Paper 2: Comparative essay on studied works

What examiners want

  • Clear argument (thesis) that answers the prompt
  • Close analysis (how language creates meaning)
  • Evidence integrated smoothly (short quotations)
  • Organized paragraphs with purposeful links

Paper 1 (unseen) strategy

Planning (fast)

  • Read once for meaning, once for technique
  • Pick 2–3 major features (tone, imagery, structure, diction)
  • Write a thesis that uses the prompt language
  • Outline 3 body paragraphs (feature → effect)

Paragraph template

  • Claim: what the writer does
  • Evidence: short quote
  • Technique: name it (imagery, contrast, syntax)
  • Analysis: effect + link to theme/prompt
  • Link: back to thesis

Paper 2 (comparative) strategy

What to prep beforehand

  • Theme banks (power, identity, conflict, belonging)
  • Quote bank by theme (5–8 quotes per work)
  • Technique bank (motifs, structure, characterization)
  • Comparative points (similarities + differences)

Essay structure

  • Intro: thesis + both works mentioned clearly
  • Body: Point → Work A → Work B → compare
  • Body paragraphs must be comparative, not two mini-essays
  • Conclusion: judgement + why the difference matters

Common mark traps

Analysis traps

  • Retelling plot instead of analyzing techniques
  • Quotes with no explanation of effect
  • Vague words: “shows”, “portrays” without how/why

Structure traps

  • No clear thesis
  • Paragraphs not linked to prompt
  • Paper 2: describing Work A then Work B (not comparing)

Study plan (English = timed writing)

  • Weekly: 1 timed Paper 1 paragraph (10–15 min) + improve it.
  • Weekly: 1 Paper 2 comparative plan (thesis + 3 points + quotes).
  • Build quote banks by theme, not by chapter/page.
  • Learn your top 5 techniques and write about them fluently.
